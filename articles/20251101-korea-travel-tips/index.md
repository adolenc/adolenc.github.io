These are some hints I am primarily writing for myself to make my next trip easier. 
This is not an exhaustive list, it only covers things that I couldn't find in other online sources or were still confusing to me as an European even after reading them.

- traveling between Incheon airport and Seoul
  - it is pretty far, like a 40 minute drive far
  - one option is to use AREX train which takes you to the main Seoul station, you can buy tickets at airport next to AREX entrance with credit card
  - another option is to use Airport limousine busses (e.g. something like number 6200), you can check which one takes you the closest to your hotel via Naver maps and you can pay the fee of 17000 won directly to the bus driver
  - for tax return at Incheon airport, use kiosks located just behind the checkin (baggage drop) terminals. The line might be very long but it goes relatively fast, about 30 minutes for the entire full line to clear. If you choose cash refund at those machines, you will need to pick that up with your passport after the security/immigration check next to I believe gate 27 for terminal 1. that line also goes pretty fast, about 10 minutes, and you can get cash in USD/KRW/RMB/YEN (no EUR!) 
- for exploring Seoul (subway, busses)
  - use either Climate card or T money card
  - you can purchase them at the convenience store at airport with credit card
  - you will need cash (physical cash only!) to fill it at "recharge stations" found in subways. I used a generic T money recharge station to fill up my Climate card (the english used on these machines might be slightly confusing)
  - often there is an ATM near-ish these stations, search for ATM on Naver maps. When filling the card up, drop it onto the tray for the card, and leave it there until you are done putting money into the machine and the machine tells you you can remove it
  - make sure to always tag your card when entering subway/bus AND when exiting
  - for busses, make sure to press the STOP button if you want to get off at the next station
  - on the bus and subway some of the chairs are "reserved" for disabled/elderly/pregnant. usually you don't sit on those period (if the bus is full just stand), but if there are a lot of seats still available and you would otherwise be standing, it is also fine to sit in them
  - during night (midnight to 5am?) the subways are closed. Bus transport is reduced to night busses with prefix N. Usually if you are not close to anything, use either bikes or taxis (just use the Uber app).
- Naver
  - don't make a Naver account or rely on it. Your account might get randomly banned for "logging in from suspicious locations" (which in my case was just the korean sim card/hotel wifi with no VPN) and they will not reinstate it for you.
  - you can and probably should use Naver for maps and navigation, it works great even without being logged in 
  - sometimes Naver search is fussy with the direct addresses. it helps to use either google maps or kakao maps and then copy the address to naver from those apps instead
- Eating
  - Naver maps is so-so for finding restaurants. sometimes it won't find a restaurant that's right there in front of you, other times it will have incorrect or incomplete working hours. it is good for pictures or reviews or sometimes for learning how to eat the food. also has a filter for finding restaurants that serve solo visitors.
  - pretty much every restaurant or cafe had a digital order kiosk with support for English language. you order there, pay right at the kiosk, sit down, get food (your number might be called in korean language though so just keep a lookout for either displays with numbers or the prep area), and when you are done carry the tray to the tray return station and clean after yourself
  - in other restaurants, you tell the receptionist how many of you there are, they sit you down, you order, you get food, and when you are done eating just walk to the cash register and pay directly there if you haven't been asked to pay at start. no tipping.
  - note that some restaurants won't serve solo travelers.
  - note the "last order" time and make sure you get to the restaurant before that
- Other things
  - buying stuff via Trazy or KoreaTravelEasy is fine. I used either to book various activities and they were all great. You might need to make a kakaotalk account to book some of these things.
  - I used Trazy to buy a KT sim card which I then picked up at the airport with the voucher sent via mail. 
  - you can pay for pretty much everything with a credit card issued abroad
  - booking.com works great
  - google translate works well enough, especially with the camera mode
  - for subway entrances, sometimes there are multiple ways to get to/from the station, and some ways will have escalators and others will only have normal stairs. There will be a lot of stair walking anyway. I think there are lifts at every subway too but I haven't used these.
  - all the subway exits are well marked with numbers
  - no jailwalking. road crossings work automatically without pressing any buttons. note that when the pedestrian crossing light turns red the car light turns green pretty much instantly, so there is no buffer and you need to be across while the light is still green. if there is no semaphore for the crossing the pedestrians have the right of way.
  - bikers/motorcyclists (food delivery) are a bit aggressive so make sure to be predictable when walking so they can avoid you
  - on escalators, the right side is if you just want to ride and left side is for walking
  - be wary of cults. Normal Koreans usually mind their own business and will pretty much never randomly talk to you. Cult people speak English very well and while it doesn't seem necessarily dangerous, you might lose a bunch of time off your travel and some small amount of money if you decide to engage with them. Just say you are not interested and they will not bother you. For some examples search around Shincheonji or Daesun Jinrihoe on google/reddit.
